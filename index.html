Case Study — 01
Intelligent Bus Management System
Real-Time Dashboard · Map UI

Students had no reliable way to track their bus — missed rides were common, waiting was anxious. The challenge: display live GPS, AI risk scores, and route data to a non-technical audience without overwhelming them.

40+Students & Drivers Interviewed
2Role-Based User Views Designed
67%Fewer Steps After Usability Testing
Information Design Live Map UI Role-Based Layout Alert States
Problem Discovery
Surveyed 40+ students and 6 drivers to identify core pain points: no live visibility, confusing paper schedules, and no early warning for route hazards. Contextual observation at bus stops revealed that stress peaked at the point of uncertainty — not at arrival delays themselves.
Information Architecture
Designed a role-based split: a student view surfacing ETAs and seat availability, and an admin dashboard for route control and risk management — so neither audience was overloaded with the other's data. Applied a strict progressive disclosure model: headline status first, detail on tap.
Map & Risk Visualisation
The AI's AHP-based risk predictions were translated into a colour-coded zone overlay (green / amber / red) on the Leaflet.js map — so users could read safety status instantly without knowing how the algorithm works. ETAs were surfaced as large, scannable numbers rather than buried in tables.
Usability Testing & Iteration
First-round testing with first-year students revealed a 6-step onboarding flow caused drop-off. After restructuring the information hierarchy, onboarding was reduced to 2 steps — a 67% reduction — without removing any core functionality.
Case Study — 02
AI Facial Recognition Bus Pass
Trust Design · Error States

Replacing physical passes with facial recognition only works if students trust it and drivers act on it in under 3 seconds. The design challenge wasn't the AI accuracy — it was the human moment at the bus door.

3Distinct System States Designed
<3sTarget Task Time at Bus Door
100%Edge Case Coverage via Override Flow
Trust UX Error State Design Microcopy Accessibility
Problem Discovery
Conducted contextual observation at the campus bus stop: poor lighting, crowds, masked students. Identified that prior systems failed not because of model accuracy, but because error feedback was incomprehensible — drivers froze, students felt accused, queues piled up.
System State Design
Defined three unambiguous visual states — Verified (green pulse + name card), Unrecognised (amber + retry prompt), and Denied (red + support code) — each with a distinct visual weight so drivers could scan without reading a single word.
Microcopy & Trust Language
Wrote empathetic, non-accusatory feedback copy: "Face not captured clearly — please face forward" instead of "Authentication Failed." Each message preserved dignity and gave users a clear next action rather than a dead end.
Accessibility & Edge Cases
Introduced a manual override flow for students with visual impairments, religious head coverings, or low-light conditions — ensuring the system degraded gracefully. Colour contrast on the verification screen was tested against direct sunlight using WCAG AA standards.
Case Study — 03
InnerAI — Mental Health Chatbot
Conversation Design · Emotional UX

Designing a mental health chatbot is among the highest-stakes UX challenges: every word, colour, and timing either builds trust or breaks it entirely. The NLP worked — the design had to make people feel safe enough to use it.

3Tier Response Architecture
SafeMessaging Guidelines Followed
0Accusatory or Minimising Copy
Conversation Design Emotional UX Safe Messaging Typography UX
Problem Discovery
Reviewed published research on chatbot abandonment in mental health apps — users quit when the bot felt robotic, patronising, or unsafe. Mapped the emotional arc of a distressed user to find where the interface needed to slow down, validate, or escalate — rather than dispense generic advice.
Conversation Architecture
Designed a 3-tier response structure: (1) empathetic acknowledgement before any guidance, (2) personalised coping strategies surfaced by the NLP sentiment layer, and (3) a graceful escalation path to professional resources when distress signals crossed a threshold — never abrupt, never alarming.
Safe Messaging & Copy Design
All in-app copy follows safe messaging guidelines: no minimising language ("just relax"), no toxic positivity, no unsolicited advice. Every response was reviewed against a plain-language checklist to ensure clarity without clinical coldness.
Visual & Motion Tone
Chose a muted, warm palette with low-contrast UI chrome to reduce visual stimulation during distress. Avoided alert-reds for emotional feedback. Set body type at 17px with generous line height. Micro-animations — typing indicators, screen transitions — were kept slow and deliberate to signal patience, not urgency.
